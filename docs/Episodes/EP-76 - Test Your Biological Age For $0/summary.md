## Overview

Bryan Johnson demonstrates how to assess biological age using seven free or near-free tests that can be performed at home, eliminating the need for expensive blood work and imaging. The video emphasizes that chronological age differs from biological age—your functional capacity—and shows that data collection and self-measurement are foundational to building an anti-aging protocol.

## Key Insights

- **Chronological vs. Biological Age**: You can be chronologically one age but functionally much younger or older depending on your fitness, strength, and mobility
- **Push-ups benchmark (30-year-old female)**: Average is 8 continuous push-ups; >16 is excellent category; equivalent to biological age 18 at 40+ reps
- **Sit-rise test (max 10 points)**: Measures core and leg strength; -1 point per touch down, -5 for unsteadiness; score of 8+ is the goal as you age
- **Sit-and-reach flexibility test**: Average for females is 19 inches; poor flexibility increases injury risk and fall risk
- **One-leg stand with eyes closed**: Average for 30-year-old is 17 seconds; strong predictor of all-cause mortality in adults over 41
- **Reaction time test**: Gets longer as you age; more pronounced decline in women than men; can be measured via free smartphone app
- **Waist-to-height ratio**: Target <0.5; preferred over BMI for measuring visceral fat; measured at narrowest point between ribs and hips without sucking in stomach
- **Grip strength**: Average ~62 lbs; "off the charts" ~91 lbs; costs $20 for dynamometer; seventh test

## Core Concepts

**Data Science Foundation**: "The secret to our success has been data science begins with counting." Measurement is the prerequisite for improvement; you can't improve what you don't measure.

**Biological Age**: A representation of your functional capacity across multiple physical domains (strength, flexibility, balance, reaction speed, body composition), rather than just years lived.

**Baseline Assessment**: Establishing where you currently stand is essential before systematic improvement; these tests provide that entry point without expensive medical equipment.

**Non-Comprehensive but Accessible**: These seven tests are not comprehensive health assessment but rather an entry point and baseline before considering blood work, imaging, and aging rate biomarkers.

## Practical Takeaways

1. **Perform the push-up test**: Do continuous push-ups with proper form (full chest-to-ground contact, full arm extension) and compare to age/gender benchmarks to establish baseline upper body endurance

2. **Measure your sit-rise score**: Attempt the floor sit-stand test (crisscross applesauce position, no hand/knee touches), tracking your score out of 10 to assess functional strength

3. **Test flexibility via sit-and-reach**: Set up a measuring tape at 15 inches and measure how far you can reach with straight legs to assess hamstring and lower back flexibility

4. **Establish balance baseline**: Stand on one leg with eyes closed for three trials to measure balance; aim for >17 seconds as a 30-year-old

5. **Use free reaction time app**: Download a reaction time/reflex app and complete 5 trials of the "lights out" game to measure response speed

6. **Calculate your waist-to-height ratio**: Measure height and waist at narrowest point (relaxed, not sucked in) and divide waist by height; target <0.5

7. **Establish a systematic improvement plan**: After getting baseline scores on all tests, pick one or two metrics to improve incrementally rather than expecting dramatic changes

## Key Learnings & Conclusions

Biological age assessment doesn't require expensive medical infrastructure—simple physical performance tests provide meaningful data on functional capacity and mortality risk. By establishing baselines on metrics like grip strength, balance, flexibility, and reaction time, anyone can identify which domains of physical function need improvement and track progress over time. The critical insight is that chronological age is less predictive of health outcomes than functional biomarkers, making regular self-testing a practical entry point to the data-driven anti-aging approach Bryan Johnson emphasizes across his research.
