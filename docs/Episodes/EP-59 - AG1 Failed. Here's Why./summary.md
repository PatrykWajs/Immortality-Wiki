## Overview

Bryan Johnson critiques AG1 (Athletic Greens) as an overpriced, over-marketed supplement that fails to deliver promised value. He contrasts AG1's practices with his own Blueprint supplement line, explaining the cost structure, marketing incentives, and ingredient transparency issues that make AG1 untrustworthy for consumers seeking genuine health optimization.

## Key Insights

- **Cost-to-consumer markup:** AG1 costs approximately $10 in raw materials but sells for $99 per purchase ($70 at doorstep), creating a 7-10x markup compared to Blueprint's $10 materials-to-$30 consumer price point
- **Influencer compensation structure:** AG1 pays influencers 20-30% commission on every sale in perpetuity; a single influencer with 31,000 subscribers can earn $500,000/month or $1 million in just 2 months
- **Proprietary blend problem:** 49 of AG1's ingredients have undisclosed dosages listed only in proprietary blends, meaning consumers don't know actual intake amounts
- **Pixie dusting:** AG1 lists ingredients in subtherapeutic doses (e.g., biotin at 330 mcg) far below clinical trial doses that produced proven benefits
- **Blueprint's transparent approach:** Every ingredient in Blueprint includes exact dosages and third-party Certificates of Analysis (COAs) posted publicly
- **Cost competitiveness:** Blueprint's ingredient costs are lower than what consumers could assemble independently

## Core Concepts

**Proprietary Blends**
A supplement labeling practice where ingredient lists are grouped together without individual dose disclosure, preventing consumers from knowing exactly how much of each compound they're consuming.

**Clinical Grade Dosing**
Using ingredient quantities that match or exceed doses proven effective in peer-reviewed clinical trials, rather than smaller "pixie dust" amounts that provide minimal therapeutic benefit.

**Pixie Dusting**
The practice of including recognized ingredients at ineffectual doses to create perceived value and credibility without delivering actual health benefits.

**Influencer Marketing Economics**
Recurring commission structures that create powerful financial incentives for endorsements regardless of product quality, often resulting in widespread promotion despite objective product shortcomings.

**Supply Chain Transparency**
Full disclosure of ingredient sourcing, testing for contamination, third-party verification, and exact formulation details to allow consumer verification of claims.

## Practical Takeaways

- **Follow the financial incentives:** When evaluating supplement or health product recommendations, research whether the recommender receives commission or payment—this often explains promotion more than efficacy
- **Demand dose transparency:** Before purchasing any supplement, verify that all ingredients list specific doses; reject products relying on proprietary blends or undisclosed amounts
- **Compare actual cost structures:** Research the manufacturing cost of supplements you're considering; if retail price is 5-10x the ingredient cost, you're likely overpaying
- **Request third-party testing documentation:** Ask manufacturers for COAs from independent labs verifying ingredient identity and purity
- **Evaluate scientific rigor:** Choose supplement brands that reference peer-reviewed clinical trials with doses matching those used in the research, not lower "pixie dust" versions

## Key Learnings & Conclusions

The supplement industry's profit incentives frequently prioritize marketing and margin expansion over ingredient efficacy and consumer value. AG1's combination of inflated pricing, undisclosed ingredient doses, and massive influencer commission structures demonstrates how financial incentives can drive endorsements independent of product quality. Blueprint's approach—clinical-grade dosing, full transparency, competitive pricing, and personal use by the founder—represents an alternative framework where scientific evidence and consumer trust align. When evaluating any health optimization product, examining cost structures and financial incentives reveals more truth than marketing claims.
