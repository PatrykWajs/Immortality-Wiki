    1. Telomere extension: Telomeres are the protective caps at the ends of chromosomes that shorten with each cell division. When telomeres become critically short, cells can no longer divide, leading to aging and eventual death. Research into telomere extension aims to prevent or reverse this process, potentially slowing down by halting aging.

    2. Senolytic: These are drugs or chemicals that selectively remove senescent cells—cells that have stopped dividing and contribute to aging-related diseases. Eliminating senescent cells may reduce inflammation, improve tissue function, and potentially extend healthy lifespans.

    3. Genetic modification: Advances in genetic engineering, such as CRISPR-Cas9 technology, could potentially be used to modify genes associated with aging or age-related diseases, potentially extending lifespans and improving overall health.

    Page 22¶

    1. Nanotechnology: The development of microscopic, self-replicating robots or nanobots could be used to repair or replace damaged cells and tissues within the body. This technology could potentially extend lifespans by maintaining the body in optimal condition at the cellular level.

    2. Brain-Computer interfaces: These interfaces aim to connect the human brain directly to digital devices or even upload a person's consciousness onto a computer. This concept, known as mind uploading, suggests the possibility of achieving a form of digital immortality by preserving one's thoughts, memories, and personality in a virtual environment.

    3. Cryonics: Cryonics is the preservation of a deceased person's body or brain at extremely low temperatures with the hope of future revival. The idea is that, as technology advances, it may become possible to repair the damage caused by aging or disease and revive the individual effectively granting them a form of immortality.
